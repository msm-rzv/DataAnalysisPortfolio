THE IMPACT OF SOCIAL MEDIA

What the data shows about how social media affects teens across mental health, academic performance, and sleep.

5.52h avg daily usage at age 19
vs 2.6 h at age 13

Social Media & Mental Health

Usage more than doubles from age 13 to 19. High users score double the anxiety of low users — 3.13 vs 1.55 — and nearly double on addiction. Depression rates climb from 11% at 13 to 25.6% at 18.

3.79 avg GPA — high users
vs 3.99 for low users

Social Media & Academic Performance

A consistent 0.20 GPA gap between high and low users — gradual but present across every usage group, enough to affect grade boundaries or scholarship eligibility.

1.49h sleep lost per night
heavy vs minimal screen users

Social Media & Sleep

Heavy bedtime screen users sleep just 6.75h vs 8.24h for minimal users — well below the recommended 8–10 hours for teenagers. Every extra hour of screen time before bed has a measurable cost.

42.9% depression rate
high SM + poor sleep

The Highest Risk Profile

Teens combining 5h+ of social media with under 6h of sleep have a depression rate of 42.9% — more than three times the 13.4% of teens with healthy habits. Poor sleep amplifies everything else.

RECOMMENDATIONS

Three interventions supported directly by the data — with tailored approaches for different age groups.

↓ 2× anxiety reduction
low vs high usage

Reduce Social Media Use

Phone-free school hours and parental screen time agreements cut daily usage at source for younger teens. For older teens, showing them the data is more effective than rules — they respond to logic, not lectures. Digital literacy classes help both groups understand how apps are designed to maximise engagement.

↓ 3× depression rate
with healthy sleep habits

Improve Sleep

No screens 1 hour before bed directly closes the 1.49h sleep gap. For younger teens, consistent enforced bedtimes work best. For older teens, framing sleep as a performance tool for grades and sport lands better than any mental health argument.

↓ 49% depression rate
active vs inactive teens

Increase Physical Activity

The single most measurable protective factor in the dataset — nearly halving depression risk. Mandatory daily PE works for younger teens. For older teens, making exercise social and competitive through after-school sports and clubs is far more effective than obligation.

Closing Note "No single intervention is sufficient. The highest-risk teens are struggling across multiple dimensions simultaneously. Addressing all three — social media, sleep, and exercise — together could meaningfully reduce the 42.9% depression rate seen in the highest-risk group."
