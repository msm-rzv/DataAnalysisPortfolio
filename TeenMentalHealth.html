Problem Statement

"How does social media usage impact the development of teenagers, and what can schools and parents do about it?"

ANALYSIS

Nine SQL queries were run across four sections to uncover patterns between social media use and teen wellbeing.

01
5.52h avg daily usage at age 19
vs 2.61h at age 13

Social Media Grows With Age

Usage more than doubles from age 13 to 19. Depression rates follow the same curve — from 11% at 13 to 25.6% at 18.

02
3.13 avg anxiety — high users
vs 1.55 for low users

Higher Usage = Higher Anxiety

High users (5h+) score double the anxiety of low users and nearly double on addiction (5.85 vs 3.01). Consistent across every usage group.

03
3.79 avg GPA — high users
vs 3.99 for low users

Grades Decline With Usage

A 0.20 GPA gap between high and low users — gradual but consistent. Enough to affect grade boundaries or scholarship eligibility.

04
1.49h sleep lost per night
heavy vs minimal screen users

Screen Time Steals Sleep

Heavy bedtime screen users sleep just 6.75h vs 8.24h for minimal users — well below the recommended 8–10 hours for teenagers.

05
42.9% depression rate
high SM + poor sleep

The Highest Risk Profile

Teens combining 5h+ social media with under 6h sleep have a 42.9% depression rate — more than three times the 13.4% of teens with healthy habits.

KEY FINDINGS

"Social media use, addiction, anxiety and depression all increase steadily with age — depression rates nearly triple from 13 to 18."

"High social media users have double the anxiety of low users — 3.13 vs 1.55 out of 10."

"Heavy screen time before bed costs teens 1.49 hours of sleep per night."

"Higher social media use is consistently associated with lower GPA — 3.79 vs 3.99 for low users."

"Poor sleep combined with high social media use produces a depression rate of 42.9% — more than three times the baseline."

"Regular exercise nearly halves the depression rate — from 29.7% to 15.1%."

RECOMMENDATIONS

Three actionable interventions — with different approaches for younger and older teens.

01
↓ 2× anxiety reduction
low vs high usage

Reduce Social Media Use

  • Phone-free school hours to cut daily usage at source
  • Parental screen time agreements — no screens after 9pm
  • For older teens: show them the data rather than imposing rules
  • Digital literacy classes — teach how apps are designed to be addictive
02
↓ 3× depression rate
healthy sleep habits

Improve Sleep

  • No screens 1 hour before bed — directly closes the sleep gap
  • For younger teens: parental enforcement of consistent bedtimes
  • For older teens: frame sleep as a performance tool for grades and sport
03
↓ 49% depression rate
active vs inactive teens

Increase Physical Activity

  • Mandatory daily PE in school schedules for younger teens
  • After-school sports and clubs — make it social for older teens
  • The single most measurable protective factor in the dataset
Closing Note "No single intervention is sufficient. The highest-risk teens are struggling across multiple dimensions simultaneously. Addressing all three — social media, sleep, and exercise — together could meaningfully reduce the 42.9% depression rate seen in the highest-risk group."
